Overview

The Land Below the Wind

Malaysian Borneo, the states of Sabah and Sarawak, sits on one of the oldest rainforests on Earth, some 130 million years in the making. Its biodiversity is staggering: wild orangutans, pygmy elephants, proboscis monkeys and the giant rafflesia flower all call this jungle home.

Crowning it all is 4,095 m Mount Kinabalu, Malaysia's highest peak and a UNESCO World Heritage Site. Base yourself in lively Kota Kinabalu, then venture out to the Sepilok orangutan sanctuary, cruise the wildlife-rich Kinabatangan River, or descend beneath the waves at Sipadan, routinely ranked among the best dive sites on the planet.

Experiences

Top Things To Do

Summit a mountain, swim with turtles and lock eyes with a wild orangutan, Borneo delivers adventure like nowhere else.

Climb Mount Kinabalu

A two-day guided trek to the 4,095 m granite summit of Low's Peak, catch sunrise above the clouds on Malaysia's highest mountain.

Sepilok Orangutan Sanctuary

Watch rehabilitated orangutans swing in to feed at the world-famous Sepilok rehabilitation centre near Sandakan.

Sipadan Diving

Plunge into one of the world's top dive sites, barracuda tornadoes, green turtles and a wall dropping 600 m into the blue.

Kinabatangan River Safari

Drift along Sabah's longest river at dawn and dusk to spot proboscis monkeys, pygmy elephants, hornbills and crocodiles.

Islands off Kota Kinabalu

Hop to the five islands of Tunku Abdul Rahman Marine Park for snorkelling, white sand and a quick escape from the city.

Sunset at KK Waterfront

End the day with Borneo's famous fiery sunsets over the South China Sea, fresh seafood and the buzzing night market in Kota Kinabalu.

Good to know

Quick Facts

Best Season

March to October brings the driest weather and best diving visibility at Sipadan. Kinabalu can be climbed year-round.

Getting There

Fly into Kota Kinabalu International Airport (BKI), about 2.5 hours direct from Kuala Lumpur, with onward flights to Sandakan and Tawau.

Currency

Malaysian Ringgit (RM). Carry some cash for rural lodges and river camps where card payment is not always available.

Language

Bahasa Malaysia is official and English is widely understood. Sabah is also home to dozens of indigenous Kadazan-Dusun communities.
